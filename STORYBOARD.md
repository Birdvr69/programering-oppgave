# ❄️ Interactive Story: Growing Up in a Changing World
## Storyboard & Planning Document

---

## STORY OVERVIEW
**Theme:** Climate change education and environmental responsibility  
**Protagonist:** Maya, a 16-year-old girl growing up in Juneau, Alaska  
**Tone:** Personal, reflective, and empowering - showing how climate change affects daily life  
**Target Audience:** Young people learning about climate change through a relatable character  
**Literary Device:** **Motif** - Recurring image of "Melting Ice" represents lost stability and changing seasons throughout the story  

---

## SCENES & NARRATIVE

### **SCENE 1: Introduction - "The Ice is Different Now"**
**Type:** Interactive (User makes first choice)

**Visual Setting:**
- Juneau, Alaska waterfront with glaciers visible in the distance
- Family home with a view of Mendenhall Glacier
- Photos on the wall showing the same glacier from 10 and 20 years ago - noticeably smaller
- Gray, cool color palette with blues

**Narrative Text:**
```
Your name is Maya. You're 16 years old, growing up in Juneau, Alaska.

This morning, you're looking at old family photos. One shows your mom standing 
at Mendenhall Glacier when she was your age. The glacier reaches all the way to 
the valley. 

In today's photo, the same glacier is half the size. Just a white smudge on the mountain.

Your little brother asks: "Why is the ice melting so fast?"

You realize you don't have a good answer. But you know something has changed.

That night, your school is hosting a climate change discussion. Do you go?
```

**Motif Reference:** Melting glacier as lost stability

**Player Choices:**
- **Option A: Attend the school discussion** → Learn more and hear what others think
- **Option B: Skip it and stay home** → Avoid the topic because it feels overwhelming

**Outcome:** Both paths lead to Scene 2, but with different starting points
- Scene 2A: Informed and motivated
- Scene 2B: Uncertain but becoming aware

---

### **SCENE 2A: "At the School Discussion"**
**Type:** Interactive (Second major choice - Personal decision)

**Visual Setting:**
- School auditorium with other teenagers
- Local climate scientist speaking with data and photos
- Images show Alaska's changing landscape over decades
- Warm lighting, engaged audience

**Narrative Text:**
```
The auditorium is packed. A climate scientist from the university shows data about 
Alaska. Sea ice is thinner. Seasons are changing. Temperature records keep breaking.

"These changes affect everything," she says. "Fishing patterns, wildlife, communities."

Your best friend Dave leans over and whispers: "This is depressing. Why is she telling us 
this? There's nothing we can do anyway."

You understand his feeling. But the scientist also talks about solutions: renewable energy, 
community action, individual choices that add up.

When the discussion ends, people are talking. Some seem scared. Some seem determined.

A classmate approaches: "I'm starting a climate club at school. Want to help?"

Do you speak up and get involved, or stay quiet?
```

**Motif Reference:** Melting ice as a metaphor for things you can no longer ignore

**Player Choices:**
- **Option A: Speak up and join the climate club** → Get involved despite fear, become an advocate
- **Option B: Stay silent and go with the crowd** → Keep quiet because it feels easier

**Outcome:** 
- Scene 3A: Engaged path (speaking up)
- Scene 3B: Uncertain path (avoiding responsibility)

---

### **SCENE 2B: "Avoiding the Conversation"**
**Type:** Interactive (Second major choice - Personal consequence)

**Visual Setting:**
- Home: Your bedroom and kitchen
- Family dinner scene
- Outside window: rain instead of predicted snow
- Colors: Gray, muted, isolating

**Narrative Text:**
```
You skip the discussion. That night at dinner, your mom mentions she read that 
Alaska's winters are 3 degrees warmer than they were 30 years ago.

"It doesn't feel that different to me," Dave says when you text him later.

But it does. You remember your elementary school photos from winter break—snow piled 
up in December. This year, December was mostly rain.

Your little brother's favorite activity is glacier hiking. But guidebooks say some 
popular trails won't even exist in 10 years. He doesn't know that yet.

You could tell him. You could talk to your family about it. You could ask what you 
can do as a household. But that feels like picking a fight.

Your mom comes home from work looking tired. Your dad is stressed about the fishing 
industry changing. Do you bring up climate change, or keep the peace?
```

**Motif Reference:** Melting ice surfaces in daily life—someone must acknowledge it

**Player Choices:**
- **Option A: Start a family conversation** → Help them understand and discuss solutions together
- **Option B: Say nothing and let it go** → Keep life normal and avoid difficult emotions

**Outcome:**
- Scene 3A: Family engagement and action
- Scene 3B: Continued avoidance and isolation

---

### **SCENE 3: "Your Path Forward"**
**Type:** Interactive (Final decision - determines ending)

**Context varies by previous choices:**

**3A - The Activist Path** (reached if Player chose "Attend discussion" + "Speak up")
```
Three months have passed. You've been meeting with the climate club twice a week. 
You've organized a fossil fuel divestment petition. You've talked to your family about 
reducing waste.

Your mom started composting. Your dad is interested in switching his truck.

But you're also feeling the weight of it. Sometimes you feel like you can't do enough. 
Your friends think you're too serious now. You're tired.

You stand on a bridge overlooking the glacier. It's smaller again this spring.

Will you:
- Keep pushing for bigger change, even when it feels impossible?
- Find balance between action and taking care of yourself?
```

**3B - The Wake-Up Call** (reached if Player chose different paths)
```
Six months have passed. You didn't join the climate club. But you did talk to your 
family about what you learned online.

Your dad cut back on driving. Your mom packed away some single-use plastics.

It doesn't feel like much. But something shifted. You realized that staying ignorant 
won't make the problem go away. It will just make you unprepared.

You're looking at colleges now. Some have strong environmental programs.

Will you:
- Choose convenience and comfort despite knowing the costs?
- Take responsibility for being part of solutions?
```

**Final Choice (determines your ending — YOUR IDENTITY as an adult):**
- **Option A: Choose responsibility and action** → Commit to building a sustainable future, even though it requires sacrifice
- **Option B: Prioritize convenience and personal comfort** → Accept change as inevitable and focus on your own life

## ENDINGS

### **THE AWAKENED PATH**
**Reached by:** Choosing engagement, speaking up, taking responsibility

**Visual Setting:**
- Maya at age 22, working as a climate scientist intern
- New solar panels on Alaskan homes
- Mixed landscape—some glaciers smaller but some valley recovery visible
- Community working together on adaptation projects
- Colors: Hopeful blues and greens with highlights of effort and change

**Narrative:**
```
Ten years later, you're 26 years old. You studied environmental science in college. 
You're now an intern at a research center in Alaska, studying glacier dynamics.

The melting hasn't stopped. Some glaciers will be gone in your lifetime. But you've 
learned something important: knowing the problem and choosing action, even imperfectly, 
is better than denial.

Your family has changed too. Your brother is in high school and writes about climate 
policy for the school newspaper—inspired by your choices.

You stand at Mendenhall Glacier again. It's smaller than the photos. But you're not 
empty. You're part of a generation that's actually doing something about it.

The ice is still melting. But so is apathy.

YOU CHOSE TO BE PART OF THE SOLUTION.

THE END - A FUTURE OF ENGAGEMENT
```

**Motif Reference:** Final image of melting ice, but paired with human action and adaptation

---

### **THE COMFORTABLE PATH**
**Reached by:** Avoiding action and choosing convenience

**Visual Setting:**
- Maya at age 22, living comfortably in a climate-controlled space
- World outside showing visible climate impacts—extreme weather, ecosystem change
- Indifferent to the landscape
- Colors: Numb grays and soft pastels, disconnected, isolated

**Narrative:**
```
Ten years later, you're 26 years old. You chose a different path.

You didn't study environmental science. You got a degree you enjoyed and landed a 
good job. Life is comfortable. You have nice things. You don't think too much about 
the melting glaciers.

But the world outside has changed in ways you can't ignore. Seasons are chaotic. 
Fires are bigger. Weather is more extreme. Your little brother can't glacier hike 
anymore—those trails are gone.

You think sometimes about that school discussion you skipped. About the climate club 
you didn't join. About the conversations you avoided at home.

You tell yourself there's nothing you could have done anyway. That one person doesn't 
matter.

But you know that's not entirely true.

THE END - A FUTURE OF CONSEQUENCE
```

**Motif Reference:** Melting ice as a reality you can no longer avoid, even through avoidance

---

### **THE BALANCED PATH**
**Reached by:** Mixed choices showing gradual awakening

**Visual Setting:**
- Maya at age 22, making thoughtful lifestyle changes
- Community-focused environmental work (not career-changing, but meaningful)
- Small victories visible in daily life
- Some climate impacts still happening, but humans adapting
- Colors: Realistic—green and gray together, effort and challenge mixed

**Narrative:**
```
Ten years later, you're 26 years old. You didn't become a climate activist. You didn't 
completely ignore the problem.

You found balance. You work in environmental education. You help teach kids about 
climate change in your hometown. You've made changes to your own life—more sustainable 
choices, less waste, conversations with people you care about.

It's not revolutionary. But it's real.

The glacier is still smaller. Alaska is still changing. But your community is adapting. 
Your family talks about climate change now. Your influence as a young person mattered 
in small ways that ripple outward.

You realize: Individual choices matter. But they also need to be part of larger 
systemic change. You're learning how to hold both realities.

The ice is melting. But so are the barriers to action.

THE END - A FUTURE OF SMALL COURAGE
```

**Motif Reference:** Melting ice as a call to action that you're learning to answer

---

### **❌ BAD ENDING**
**Reached by:** Choosing "Ignore the problem" + "Choose convenience"

**Visual Setting:**
- Arctic without ice
- Barren, dark landscape
- Dead or migrating wildlife
- Colors: Dark browns, grays, blacks - bleak and hopeless

**Narrative:**
```
The ice is gone. The Arctic is unrecognizable.

Humans ignored the warnings and continued to pollute. Emissions soared. 
The climate spiraled. The ice that took thousands of years to form melted 
in just decades.

The polar bear and millions of species lost their home. Arctic ecosystems 
collapsed. The world became hotter, less stable, and more hostile.

The bear's story ends.

But humanity's story... continues.

THE END - A WARNING FOR OUR WORLD
```

## VISUAL DESIGN GUIDELINES

### **Color Palette by Scene**

| Scene | Primary Colors | Mood |
|-------|---|---|
| Scene 1 | Deep blue, white, light gray | Cold, desperate |
| Scene 2A | Brown, tan, dark green | Melting, unstable |
| Scene 2B | Steel blue, dark gray, white | Fragile, stormy |
| Good Ending | Bright blue, green, yellow | Hopeful, alive |
| Mixed Ending | Muted blue, gray, tan | Uncertain, changing |
| Bad Ending | Dark brown, black, gray | Bleak, lost |

### **Typography**
- **Title/Headings:** Bold, modern sans-serif (e.g., Montserrat, Poppins)
- **Body Text:** Clean, readable sans-serif (e.g., Roboto, Inter)
- **Buttons:** Medium weight, clear and clickable

### **Animation Ideas**
- Fade-in/fade-out between scenes
- Melting ice effect (visual transition)
- Glowing buttons for interactive choices
- Slow background movement (drifting ice, migration)
- Text typewriter effect for dramatic moments

## TECHNICAL STRUCTURE

### **Scenes List for Coding**
```
totalScenes = 6 main scenes + 3 endings
  - Scene 1: Introduction
  - Scene 2A: Land journey
  - Scene 2B: Ice journey
  - Scene 3A: Improving environment
  - Scene 3B: Uncertain environment
  - Scene 3C: Worsening environment
  - Scene 3D: Collapsed environment
  - Ending: Good
  - Ending: Mixed
  - Ending: Bad
```

### **Data Structure (JavaScript)**
Each scene should contain:
```javascript
{
  id: "scene1",
  title: "The Last Ice",
  text: "narrative text here...",
  image: "img/scene1.jpg",
  choices: [
    { text: "Search on land", nextScene: "scene2a" },
    { text: "Hunt on remaining ice", nextScene: "scene2b" }
  ]
}
```

### **Variables to Track**
- `currentScene` – which scene is playing
- `playerChoices` – array of all decisions made
- `emissionChoice` – track attitude toward climate action
- `environmentalDamage` – scale of 0-100 for final outcome
